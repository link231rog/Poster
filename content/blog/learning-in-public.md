I used to think writing about my work was a distraction from the work itself. Turns out, it's the opposite.

## The Feedback Loop

When you write about what you're building, three things happen:

1. **You clarify your thinking.** Writing forces you to organize fuzzy ideas into concrete arguments.
2. **You attract collaborators.** People who care about the same problems find you.
3. **You build a record.** Six months later, you can look back and see how your thinking evolved.

## What "Learning in Public" Actually Means

It's not about posting every thought. It's about:

- Sharing what you're working on **before** it's polished
- Being honest about what you don't know
- Documenting your process, not just your results

> The goal isn't to look smart. It's to get smarter faster.

## How I Do It

- **This blog** - technical writing about things I'm building
- **GitHub** - open-source projects and experiments
- **Short-form posts** - quick notes on X/Twitter

The key is consistency, not volume. One thoughtful post a month beats daily noise.

## Why It Matters for Research

In research, your ideas are your currency. Writing about them publicly:

- Establishes priority and ownership
- Invites critique that improves the work
- Creates a portfolio of thinking, not just output

I'm still learning how to do this well. But I'm doing it in public.
